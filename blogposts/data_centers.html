Could the AI Bubble Popping Be Good News for Science?

July 2026

A common objection to comparing the AI bubble to the dot-com crash is that fiber optic cable was reusable infrastructure and AI data centers are not. The argument goes: when those companies went bankrupt and left cable in the ground, the cable still worked. Racks of aging GPUs are different. They are expensive to run, they depreciate fast, and they are useless once the AI industry moves on.

I think this misses something important. The compute itself is reusable infrastructure, just not for the AI industry. For scientific research it is perfectly capable hardware. The objection only holds if you assume that commercial AI is the only workload that matters. It is not.

The gap nobody is talking about

Meta just announced it is building a cloud business to sell off excess GPU capacity. It spent up to $145 billion on compute in 2026 alone and does not need all of it. AI companies are actively retiring NVIDIA A100 GPUs because they are two full generations behind the hardware needed for frontier model training today.

At the same time, the University of Minnesota's flagship supercomputing cluster, Agate, runs on AMD EPYC Milan processors from 2021 and NVIDIA A100 GPUs from 2020. That is one of the best-resourced research computing facilities in the US.

The hardware that commercial operators consider economically obsolete is the same generation that a major research university considers a modern production system. The table below makes this gap concrete.

The power cost argument cuts both ways

The reason commercial operators discard hardware is not that it stopped working. It is that newer chips produce dramatically more compute per watt on the same power budget. Once that efficiency gap is large enough, running old hardware in a commercial data center becomes a money-losing proposition even at very low rental rates. H100 rental prices have already collapsed from over $8 per GPU-hour in 2023 to around $2.50 today, and they will keep falling as Blackwell supply scales.

But universities with subsidized power and existing cooling infrastructure do not face the same math. A node that is uneconomical for CoreWeave is not uneconomical for a fluid dynamics group with a DOE allocation and a Slurm queue full of jobs. The commercial case for retiring hardware and the scientific case for acquiring it are not in conflict. They point in the same direction.

The hardware that commercial operators consider economically dead is the same generation that universities consider a modern production system.

Researchers are built for this

There is another reason the research community is well placed to absorb this hardware. Scientific programmers are unusually good at getting performance out of constrained systems. We optimize code obsessively because we have to: HPC allocations are competitive, queue times are real, and nobody gives you more cycles just because your code is inefficient. Generations of researchers have built production simulation codes on whatever hardware they could get, often years behind the commercial frontier.

Fluid dynamics, finite element analysis, and molecular dynamics codes are memory-bandwidth and core-count bound. They do not care about transformer engines or FP8 tensor cores. A 2021 EPYC node with 128 cores and 512 GB of RAM is genuinely excellent hardware for that class of work, regardless of what year it is.

Someone has to be ready to catch it

The fiber optic case is still instructive, even if the analogy is imperfect. When the dot-com bubble burst, bankrupt companies sold cable at pennies on the dollar through normal liquidation processes. It redistributed through secondary markets and eventually became the backbone of the modern internet. Nobody planned it. It happened because the infrastructure was still useful and buyers emerged.

AI hardware will not redistribute on its own timetable the way fiber did, because it has a carrying cost. It needs power and cooling just to sit idle, and it depreciates fast. The window where it is cheap enough to be worth acquiring but capable enough to be worth running is probably three to five years at most.

That means universities and research funding agencies need to be thinking about this now, not after the distressed sales begin. NSF, the DOE Office of Science, and European equivalents like the EuroHPC Joint Undertaking should be developing frameworks for acquiring decommissioned commercial hardware. University CIOs should be watching the secondary market. The case for a coordinated public acquisition strategy is real and underappreciated.

The compute is coming. The question is whether anyone in the research community is positioned to catch it, or whether it gets scrapped because nobody was paying attention.
